ABOUT

Yousef Abu-Salah is a visual storyteller and manga artist dedicated to amplifying refugee narratives through sequential art. Born in Brandon, Mississippi, and educated at Vanderbilt University, his work bridges Eastern and Western artistic traditions to create powerful visual testimonies of displacement and resilience.


Currently traveling across continents, Yousef documents and illustrates the stories of displaced communities, transforming personal testimonies into compelling manga narratives. His unique approach combines journalistic rigor with artistic sensitivity, creating works that honor the complexity of refugee experiences while making them accessible to global audiences.


Through his art, Yousef explores themes of identity, belonging, and the universal human desire for home. Each piece serves as both historical document and artistic expression, preserving stories that might otherwise go untold. His work has been featured in various exhibitions and publications, contributing to important conversations about migration, human rights, and the power of visual narrative.


When not drawing, Yousef continues to engage with communities around the world, learning languages, sharing meals, and listening to stories that inform and inspire his artistic practice.

The boat rocked gently against the Mediterranean waves. Sarah clutched her daughter's hand, feeling the small fingers tremble—not from cold, but from the same uncertainty that gripped every soul aboard. Forty-three strangers bound by a single hope: that tomorrow would find them on solid ground. "Mama, will we see the stars tonight?" Amira whispered. Sarah looked up. Despite everything, the stars remained constant—the same constellations her grandmother had taught her, now guiding them toward an unknown shore. "Yes, habibti. The stars are coming with us." Behind them, an old man began humming a melody Sarah recognized from her childhood. One by one, others joined in, their voices creating a fragile harmony against the vast darkness of the sea...
